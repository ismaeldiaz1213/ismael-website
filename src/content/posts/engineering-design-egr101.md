This is the class where you learn... how to engineer?

## TLDR

* You learn several tools used to brainstorm and prototype
* Very small and short introductions to woodworking, CAD, circuits, and casting and molding
* You learn that working in a group can be a pain but can also create long-lasting friendships

## What You Actually Learn

I genuinely don’t remember a ton of specific details from this course beyond what I mentioned in the TLDR. I’m writing this before boarding a flight, so I can’t really go digging for specifics and honestly, I never really found myself using most of this again while at Duke.

Does that say more about me or the course? I’ll let you decide. But broadly, these were the “topics”:

* Pugh Matrices
* Scoring Matrix
* Brainstorming methods
* "Tools Mastery/Tool Bits"
* Big project!
* Doing Good Presentations

## What Makes It Hard

A lot of things. But let’s start with...

### The first half

Getting through the first half of the course can feel pretty slow. There’s a lot of… talking. And a lot of sitting.

I had this class in the POD, so most of the time you’re on those stools for three hours, mainly listening. A typical day (at least for me) looked something like:

* Yap with group members until class starts
* Quick reminder about CHEESE (this safety acronym you shall never forget)
* Professor talks about something
* Time passes…
* Wait—there’s an activity in the journal? Did I bring it today? What are we doing again?
* Share with the class
* Back to lecture
* Time to go
* Leave wondering what exactly just happened, then climb the 30,000 LSRC stairs

It’s not that nothing is happening it's just sometimes feels like the pace doesn’t match how hands-on the class is supposed to be.

### The Quizzes

Every week you watch 2–3 videos and take a quiz on them. These are meant to act as pre-lecture prep, which makes sense in theory.

In practice, though, some of these videos can be *long*. Like… long enough where you start negotiating with yourself about whether to watch or just read the transcript. They’re helpful to some extent, but the time commitment can feel a bit heavy for what you get out of them.

### The project half

At some point, things shift. People present project ideas, you ask questions, and then you rank your preferences. The presenters are great, and it’s actually interesting to see the range of ideas.

After that, you get assigned a group and start your very long project journey. I talk more about mine in the projects section (assuming I link it here eventually), but this is where the course becomes much more engaging.

### The technical reports

There’s a lot expected here, with not a lot of space to work with. Our project had tons of content—tables, figures, explanations. Fitting everything in was a difficult challenge. The grading felt strict, but to be fair, the feedback was *usually* clear and helpful. Though, perhaps in the real world,  a manager at an engineering firm would have overlooked this feedback.

## What Makes It Special

When I took the class, this was called “tools mastery.” You pick between things like 3D printing, casting and molding, circuits, woodworking, and laser cutting.

The goal is to get you comfortable enough with at least one toolset to use it in your project. I chose:

### 3D printing

I came in with a decent amount of experience already (high school class + Fusion 360 certification), so this wasn’t new for me.

That said, the staff do a solid job introducing Fusion in a pretty short time. You learn enough to navigate the UI, sketch, and extrude. The assignment involves printing a few objects, and honestly, it was fun—my first (of many) prints, and I still have the keychain.

### Casting and Molding

This was probably my favorite part. The process is well-structured, and it’s satisfying to see your mold come together—even if a lot of it is just waiting for things to dry.

Once it works, it *really* works and you can make multiple copies of your design. Just... get the bubbles out. Otherwise, things get a little *w e i r d*.

## Who Should Take It

If you want my honest answer? It probably depends on what you’re looking for. If you’re hoping for a super hands-on, fast-paced engineering experience right away, this might feel a bit slow at times. But if you’re new to engineering or want a broad introduction to tools and teamwork, I can see the value.

I imagine the goal of the course is something like:

* Introduce foundational design thinking
* Expose students to different tools
* Build collaboration skills early on

And to be fair, those aren’t bad goals at all. The question is more about how effectively they land for different people.

If I had to suggest improvements, they’d mostly be around:

* Making the first half more interactive: It does not need to take 6 weeks to explain the engineering design process. 2 weeks max if we're stretching it. Enough for drop/add. Shortening this half gives more time to work on projects! Time here is valuable.
* Streamlining or shortening pre-lecture content: Some videos were the equivalent of "this could have been an email". I would actually rather just have a 2 page pdf that I read than a 5-10 minute video with a quiz. Though perhaps others disagree.
* Giving a bit more flexibility in technical report constraints: Page limits feel silly here. Especially because some projects are more complex than others (yeah I said it!). There's no reason why a mobile ventilator project is constrained the same as... well, let's call them arts and crafts projects.

## Final Take

I’ve been a bit critical throughout this, but the class definitely had its fun moments. The POD staff and professor clearly put in effort to support students (though I really wouldn't expect much from your mentors), and the project portion especially has its highlights. At the same time, there were parts that didn’t really click for me. It was mainly the pacing and how much time was spent in lecture versus doing. 

That said, this could also just be a case of personal fit. I know plenty of people who genuinely enjoyed EGR 101 and got a lot out of it. I just wasn’t one of them.

Apparently though, you could just skip this class and substitute it with another. Had I known this (and had this hindsight), I probably would have skipped it. But I got through it, lost some sleep, learned a few things, and moved on. And perhaps, that's all that matters to you. If so, have fun. You may meet friends who will stick with you for your entire college career.
